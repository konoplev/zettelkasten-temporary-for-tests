# What is Logarithm?
A logarithm is a mathematical function that calculates the power to which a number (called the base) must be raised in order to produce a given value. The base is typically represented by the symbol "b" and the value by the symbol "x".
A logarithm can be defined with respect to a base (b) where the base b-logarithm of X is equal to **y** because X equals to the b to the power of y (log(X) =y because X = b ʸ). You can take any positive number as the base of the logarithm but the most commonly used bases are —

-   Base 2 — the base 2 logarithm of 8 is 3, because 2³ = 8
-   Base 10 — the base 10 logarithm of 100 is 2, because 10² = 100
-   Natural Log — the base of the natural log is the mathematical constant “e” or Euler’s number which is equal to 2.718282. So, the natural log of 7.389 is 2, because e² = 7.389.

The **logarithm** is the [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") to [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation").  So the more you add the slower it grows. 

[Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition"), [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication"), and [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation") are three of the most fundamental arithmetic operations. The inverse of addition is subtraction, and the inverse of multiplication is [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)"). Similarly, a logarithm is the inverse operation of [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation").

One of the applications is the [[logarithm scale]].
